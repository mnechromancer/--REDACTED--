# Setting Bible — Site-41B and the Receiving Site

Original canon for the game's setting, rewritten for the **v3 frame** (2026-07-04,
`planning/reset_v3_intake.md`): the player is no longer *at* Site-41B — they receive its
documents. Per the v3 decisions (`planning/amber_build_decisions.md` §"v3"), the
**concept-level canon below survives**; the **entry-level detail written against it (the old
cluster tables, the 25-entity roster, the authored arcs) is retired and will be re-authored**
in the content pass. Where a section says *detail owed*, that is deliberate: the concept is
canon, the particulars are not yet.

Altitude rule unchanged: this doc carries themes, places, cast, and cosmology — never
individual entries. Everything below sits on the shipping side of the licensing wall
(`discovery/04_site41_antimemetics.md`): every name, anomaly, resolution, and coinage is
original.

---

## 1. The two sites

### 1.1 The receiving site (the player's — new, detail owed)
A low-prestige Foundation **records satellite** — an administrative filing station, not a
containment site. It holds no anomalies, runs a skeleton staff, and exists so that paperwork
generated elsewhere has somewhere to be. Its one distinction is negative: it is the site
that was assigned the Site-41B intake problem, because it could be spared. Senior staff
burned months on the batches; everything they produced erased at 4 PM; the task was
delegated down to the temp pool, who are cheap, replaceable, and not expected to succeed.

The player is that temp: a term-contract archivist with a login, a desk, and a supervisor
who communicates by mail and does not want updates.

*Settled (Phase 2, 2026-07-05):* the site is **Site-81C, regional records satellite** —
the "C" is the genealogy said out loud: a derivative of a derivative, a copy-station
processing a copy-site's mail. The 4 AM batch arrives as a **leased-line burst spooled to
tape**: the night equipment mounts the reel at 0400, and at 1600 the reel reads back
blank. The reference shelf carries **REF designations** (REF-01…06); it is the station's
own property and the erasure has never touched it. The receiving-site cast is registered
in §4.2.

### 1.2 Site-41B (the sender — concept canon, survives)
A decommissioned **molybdenum mine in the Colorado Front Range**, converted in 1953 to a
hardened deep-records repository: Deep Archive and records annex to Site-41. The archive
descends in **Stacks** (Stack I at the adit, Stack V at the deepest gallery). Its function,
nominal: cold storage and duplicate documentation. Its function, actual: nobody has
confirmed in years.

**The Transfer (1968)** — referenced everywhere, described nowhere: a records-custody
reorganization after which Site-41 stopped acknowledging correspondence. Requisitions
return receipt stamps from no registered office; funding continues through an appropriation
line no directorate can locate. Site-41B is the annex whose parent went quiet — the "B" is
the thesis: a derivative site, a copy of record, that outlived its original.

**Under v3, Site-41B itself is never visited and does not answer.** The batches arrive on
schedule from a site that, as far as anyone can confirm, no longer picks up the phone. The
player meets Site-41B only as its paperwork — which is the game's whole register.

### 1.3 The defining lack (survives — it is the corpus's shape)
Site-41B never received the countermeasures richer sites use against informational
anomalies. It fought record-eating phenomena **blind, with procedure as its only prosthetic
memory**: triplicate forms because three copies decay slower than one; reading rotas because
a text read aloud weekly holds its referent; cross-referencing as a survival practice. This
is why the corpus the player receives is the most redundancy-obsessed document graph in the
Foundation — a site that knew its records were its mind built exactly the dense, interlinked
web the game needs.

### 1.4 Period register (survives)
Game-present is the early 1980s, Series-I register throughout: carbon paper, microfiche,
teletype, monochrome terminals. The Anomaly Classification System does not exist; nothing
anachronistic ships. **AMBER** (Archive Management & Batch Entry Resource) is the
Foundation-standard catalog OS of the era — the receiving site runs it a decade past
deprecation. AMBER's legitimate cross-reference toolset, the **Concordance**, was built at
Site-41B under Dr. Sze to hold the archive together during the migration the Transfer
froze. AMBER does not lie; it only refuses to make things easy.

## 2. The batch and the erasure (the v3 premise)

- **4:00 AM:** a batch of heavily-redacted Site-41B documents arrives. Batches are
  cumulative — what came before comes again, plus more. Nobody transmits acknowledgments;
  nobody has reached the sender.
- **4:00 PM:** the batch and every note taken about it — written or digital — erases
  completely, by a mechanism no investigation has characterized. The erasure reaches paper.
  It reaches carbon copies. It reached, once, a chalkboard.
- **The exception:** a redacted value **re-derived inside AMBER's citation ledger** —
  the word produced, the grounding cited, the commit transmitted before the deadline —
  persists. Annotation dies; re-derivation survives. The site's working theory is a
  transmittal-protocol quirk. The truth is stranger: a properly-cited reconstruction is
  the one artifact the erasure cannot claim, and no one at the receiving site has asked
  hard enough why. *(This is the transmittal model, decision v3-A — the mechanical
  persistence rule and the lore are the same fact.)*
- **Why temps succeed where seniors failed:** the seniors produced annotation — summaries,
  cross-index cards, months of marginal notes. All of it wiped. Understanding is the only
  thing that persists in a person, and cited re-derivation is the only thing that persists
  in the system. The game's knowledge-progression layer is this fact made playable.

## 3. Sender-side canon — the surviving concepts

These survive as *concepts*; their entry-level expression is re-authored in the content
pass. The old detailed cluster specs live in `archive/` for mining, not for reuse as-is.

- **The anomaly registers.** The corpus biases informational / memetic / perceptual
  anomalies — records errors that are the anomaly (the Misfiled); org-chart holes with
  payroll (the Quiet Departments); spaces present in inventory but absent from blueprints
  (the Negative Stacks); the site's own countermeasures, never resolved as anomalous or
  not (the Retention Methods); and the high-tier discovery that archive and territory are
  coupled (the Drift). These five *registers* survive as the corpus's tonal palette — the
  clusters as content plans are retired.
- **The retention methods.** Triplicate, the reading rota, **fixative ink** mixed to a
  recipe with one illegible step, Sze's concordance experiments. Concept survives: the
  site's rituals *work*, and they are why the record can still be reconstructed at all.
- **The cosmology.** Reality is itself an archive — a maintained record whose maintenance
  lapsed. Entropy is deaccession; the deep liminal spaces are the discard pile, where
  withdrawn reality is shelved. What Sze's concordance program touched, by cross-referencing
  the records past a critical density, was the original catalog — untended, running on
  automatic. What answered is the entity (`scp_x_bible.md`). The Foundation's clerical
  compulsion was never pathology; it is the last functioning local instance of the
  universe's own method.
- **The genre statement.** Paperwork is how spaces remember what they were for; liminal
  space is what paperwork looks like after everyone stops reading it. Site-41B is the genre
  caught at the transition, fighting to stay on the read side of the line. Under v3 the
  player fights the same fight from outside, twelve hours at a time.
- **The area arc.** The mountain around the mine — the access road, the played-out
  boomtown, the resurvey country, the watershed, the claim and the deed. Extraction
  followed by deaccession: the cosmology in geological form. Survives as a content
  direction for inbound batches.
- **The liminal-lineage rule.** Kinship, never citation: infinite-interior dread inverted
  as *document without space*; sterile-water spaces as the Wet Stacks where the oral record
  drowns; maintained-enclosure horror as care, perfectly executed, for nothing confirmable.
  No lineage's names, entities, or rules ship.

## 4. Cast

### 4.1 Site-41B cast (concepts survive; they exist only in the documents)
The player never meets these people; they meet their filings, memos, and marginalia. A
small cast under heavy reuse is what makes the corpus one site rather than an anthology.
Functions survive; their authored expression is owed anew:

- **Director I. Vogel** — the institutional voice; memoranda signed in fixative ink; no
  staff file, no office on any current blueprint.
- **Archivist R. Halloran** — the predecessor thread: date-stamped marginalia, increasingly
  urgent, from someone who ran this same decipherment and is gone in a way no file states.
  Under v3, Halloran was at *Site-41B* — the warning voice arrives inside the batch.
- **A. Marsh, Custodian** — on every shift roster since 1953, never the same handwriting
  twice. The files are calmer about Marsh than about anything else.
- **Dr. E. Sze, "the Cataloguer"** — founding records officer; author of the retention
  methods and the concordance program; removed or promoted or absorbed in 1968 (all three
  verbs appear in different files).
- **Liaison T. Andrade (Site-41)** — countersignature on every pre-1968 manifest; the
  parent-site hole given a name.

### 4.2 Receiving-site cast (named Phase 2 — lives only in mail)
- **P. Redding, Records Supervision** — the supervisor who delegated the task downward and
  wants it off their desk. Terse, put-upon; points at the work, conspicuously never says
  how to do it (nobody knows how). The onboarding and pacing voice.
- **L. Ferro** — the opposite-shift temp the player never meets. Reserved for day-2+ mail:
  a peer voice first, a channel for late-game wrongness after. Not yet used; do not spend
  the name casually.
- **Personnel / Facilities** — automatic and anonymous respectively; the mundane register
  that makes the station real.

Rule unchanged: the receiving cast never appears in the corpus; the 41B cast never appears
in mail.

## 5. The redactor (survives — reserved, unwritten)

A second entity, distinct from Quippy: **the one that made the redactions.** Quippy offers
to *fill* the gaps; the redactor *made* them. Why it obscured the record — protection,
concealment, predation, or the site's own procedure escalated into an entity — stays open
and deliberately unwritten. Do not author it, name it, or give it a file; seed it only as
redacted *surface* — the shape of a hand in what is missing. It is the player's slow
second inference, behind the first one about Quippy.

## 6. Constraints (binding)

- **Licensing wall:** flavor may echo canon as heavily as wanted; every `truth:` and every
  resolution is original; nothing verbatim ships.
- **Period:** Series-I register, early 80s, no ACS, no anachronism.
- **Cast discipline:** recurring names only from §4; coin no new recurring figures without
  registering them here first.
- **Negative space is load-bearing:** Site-41's silence, the redactor, the erasure's
  mechanism — hold them open. The game is about reading gaps; the canon keeps some.
