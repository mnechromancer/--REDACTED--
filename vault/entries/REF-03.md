# REF-03 — Records Practice Manual (excerpt)

## Chapter 4 — Copying disciplines

The Foundation files its records of anomalous holdings in **triplicate**: three copies,
struck together, distributed to separate stacks. The discipline is older than any current
directive and is retained on its merits — three copies decay slower than one, disagree
more legibly than two, and survive most of the accidents that take a single copy whole.
Where an order requires a lot "filed by the standard copying discipline," the discipline
meant is this one. The copies are compared at audit; a discrepancy between them is not a
clerical failure but a finding, and is reported rather than reconciled.

## Chapter 7 — Retention and disposition

A **retention order** fixes how long a lot is held and by what method. Retention is not
storage; it is the active practice of keeping a record legible — re-inking, re-reading,
re-filing on schedule. A lot under retention order is walked past on rounds. A lot merely
stored is already leaving.

Disposition language on a retention order binds every record it enumerates. An order that
names its lot governs the lot's copies wherever they sit.

## Chapter 11 — The catalogue ledger

Records stations operate the Foundation-standard catalogue system (AMBER, all current
revisions). Two entries in its ledger are distinguished and staff must not confuse them:

An **annotation** is a note about a record — a summary, a marginal gloss, a working guess.
Annotation carries no citation and no standing. It is working material, and the ledger
treats it as disposable.

A **reconstruction of record** is a value entered *with citation*: the operator states the
value and stakes the passage, in a held record, where the value stands in the clear. The
ledger judges the citation at commit. A reconstruction that commits is transmitted with
the station's returns and has the standing of the record it was drawn from.

The distinction is procedure, not preference. Annotation is what an operator believes;
reconstruction is what the record can be made to say again. Only the second survives the
station's transmittal cycle.
